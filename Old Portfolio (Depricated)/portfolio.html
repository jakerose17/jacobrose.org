Upgrading my 3D printer

I have been enthralled with 3D printing ever since discovering it at age 14. I used profits from my small business to purchase a cheap kit to build a 3D printer. When I wanted to make dual-color and dual-material 3D prints, I knew that my current printer would not suffice. However, dual extruder 3D printers were prohibitively expensive, so I upgraded my printer to add a second extruder. I purchased a new nozzle assembly, second stepper motor, and a new motherboard to add a new axis of control. I designed and 3D printed adapting hardware and assembled the printer (shown in pictures). After assembly, I modified the firmware to provide control over the extra motor and nozzle. Successfully integrating every part was challenging. With many individual settings that had to be tuned, I dedicated 2-3 weeks to correcting the software and hardware to produce quality 3D prints.

SeaPerch: Underwater Robotics/ROV

SeaPerch is an underwater robotics competition in which I competed during the last five years. Over that time I worked as lead engineer to develop a completely 3D-printed design that improved upon the standard PVC design. This image showcases many generations of designs from back left to front right. One of the most innovative components of our design is a 3D printed gearbox for vertical thrust. To reduce cross-sectional area and horizontal drag, I designed a 3D printed right-angle gearbox and propeller assembly, seen partially mounted in the last two generations. Developing the gearbox was a challenge, as we were constrained by size and strength of 3D printed parts. Many more iterations of design than shown were required to produce a strong and efficient gearbox. Another issue we encountered was developing the flotation. The body designs depicted in this image show our process of tuning the neutral buoyancy.

SeaPerch CAD View

This is an exploded view of the final generation of our SeaPerch robot. We designed the body to print in two parts and have places to mount a weighted inferior hook and superior float to maintain stability in the water. Cavities for motors and channels for wiring can be seen in the horizontal cross-section. This allowed the robot to be extremely hydrodyamic and therefore have a very high top speed. We had to carefully design the body to be able to be waterproofed after it was 3D-printed. For example, we included channels on the outside for zip-ties to secure the body rather than screws because the screw holes were unable to be sufficiently sealed with wax.

SeaPerch in Action

These are two images of our vehicle underwater. The first is an early generation to test stability and maneuverability in the water. The second image is from an early competition. This shows the robot lifting a weighted ball through obstacles in the water. In this event, most teams at this competition could not lift these balls as their designs could not handle the extra weight. Using the same motors as other teams, we were able to complete the challenge by carefully designing our hook and center of buoyancy. When the vehicle lifted the weight, its pitch would adjust to allow all three motors to provide a component of vertical thrust.

Research in Chemistry Automatic Filament Winder

For my independent research project developing a variable-density, bioactive, and biodegradable 3D printer filament for bone reconstructive implants, I needed specialized machinery to manufacture a polymer filament that was able to be assayed. With funding constraints on my research, I could not purchase an automatic 3D printer filament winder. This winder not only stored filament after extrusion, but was integral to creating a constant filament diameter. I coded an Arduino microcontroller to accept inputs from 3 photoresistors and automatically control a motor. This image shows the electronics as I developed the software and constructed the circuit.

Research in Chemistry Project in Action

This video shows the filament extruder kit I assembled and the filament winder that I designed and created in action. As the plastic is melted and extruded into a filament, my automatic winder kept a consistent tension on the filament to create a constant diameter. The 3 photoresistors as seen in the video act as "eyes", seeing how much filament is being produced and adjusting the winding rate as needed.

Science Olympiad Gravity Vehicle

One of my events that I competed in on my Varsity Science Olympiad team was Gravity Vehicle. In the event, we were tasked to design a vehicle that could stop within millimeters of a random target distance while taking the least amount of time. For this event, I used my computer-aided-design experience to create a 3D printed body which had the ability to finely tune the angle of the wheels and contained bearings to improve speed. I also designed a track for a winged nut to count the number of rotations of the wheels to determine the distance traveled. The lightness and strength of this 3D printed design allowed my team to complete the event in less time and with greater accuracy.

Art and Technology Flower

I had the opportunity to take the course "Art and Technology" at my high school. This course used 3D printing and laser cutting to create pieces of art. For my final project, I wanted to push myself in computer-aided-design and laser cutting, so I decided to create a flower with petals at varying angles. NC Science Fair 2021-State Competition

For the 2021 North Carolina Science Fair, my partner and I developed a modular and environmentally-friendly oil filtration system for PVC drains. I modeled the system in computer-aided-design and the render is shown on the right of the image. It contains a central volume to hold the natural oil filter and threads to be easily mounted and replaced on PVC pipe. This system can be easily retrofitted to existing drains to capture oil pollution. As a state finalist, the project was chosen for the RICOH Sustainable Development Award and the Yale Science and Engineering Award.
